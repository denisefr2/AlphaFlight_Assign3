Denise Freiburger: Story Outline

Aran [Ah rahn] is a world of contrasts. From the warm marshy swamplands of Ecarnas [Ek cahr nars], to the high cold peaks of the Hilmeran [Hel mar ahn] alps and the temperate seaside flats of Aurybar [Au reeh bar]. Each continent is host to races grown accustomed to the unique challenges presented by the environment, be they natural or unnatural.

Aran is a peaceful land with slowly increasing rumblings of discontent. The orcs eye the lush farmlands of Aurybar with envy. Their recently made allies, the Goblins, hold a long standing grudge against the human interlopers and are happy to join forces with the stronger Orcs. The dragons have recently woken from their long hibernation to find their mountain home claimed by the elves. The look to reclaim what was lost. On the plains of Aurybar, the humans ever seek to increase their landholdings, often at the cost of their neighbours goodwill. The goblins they can manage, but its less certain they will survive the wrath of the sirens.

Which conflict the player becomes involved in will depend on their starting city. How they are treated by both their own countrymen and the other races, is decided by their race, their class and their starting city. Elves view dragonkin with hostility because of their blood link to the newly woken dragons. Human will treat halfers with suspicion following the treaty the halflings have made with the goblins along with their shared racial heritage. All of these and more will give unique choices and dialogue along the journey.

The player will at first be exposed at a micro level, the effects of the increasing unrest on the local village. This view will then broaden as the player continues their journey, meeting new people, interacting with other races and hearing their stories.

Its intended that the player will come to sympathise with the situations faced by all races, making the decisions faced in the later game, more and more difficult. There will not necessarily be a ‘right’ decision.

Ultimately the decisions made by the player will decide the fate of Aran for good or for ill.

Denise Freiburger: Target Audience

Cat-a-wall is intended to be an ‘all ages’ experience. Language and content should neither talk down to children nor be too simplistic for adults.

There is no intent to include online gambling, an in-game cash shop, graphic violence or online interaction with others. Any combat the player engages in should not create any lasting impact. It is intended the violence will be depicted with a similar level of detail as, for example, Civilisation 3 (Australian Classification, Civilisation 3, 2001).

The intended focus of the game is not solving issues via the medium of violence but using negotiation, cooperation and debate to learn differing viewpoints and reach a middle ground where possible. We are aware that players enjoy engaging in challenging combat and will provide the opportunity for them to do so without compromising our vision.

The completed product, when submitted for classification, should easily fall into either a “G” or “PG” rating.

Jennafer Allen: Marketing: Merchandise

Cat-A-Wall prides itself in being a free to use game that is as good as any paid for competitor. However, the reality of building a game is simple, it is expensive. As such, Alpha Flight has chosen to solicit it’s funding through the sale of merchandise. Teaming up with Printify, Alpha Flight has developed some proof of concept ideas to include a mouse pad, phone cases and male/female shirts.

For baseline funding all merchandise will include the Cat-A-Wall logo. Once characters are finalised and their popularity is better understood (for better targeted sales) the merchandise will start of as is below. A breakdown of the costs can also be found below. For further expansion the sale of items including mouse, headsets, monitors, and consoles need to be conducted later in the development cycle as equipment of that nature will have a higher start-up cost.

Partnerships with gaming equipment manufactures could be another way to lessen the burden of start-up funding whilst remaining as a source of revenue. The sale of all merchandise will be available via the game website and all social media accounts.

Zhaohe Lin: Platform Developer (Using Unreal Engine 4)

My goal this week is to explore Unreal Engine 4, familiarising myself with the game design and development process to provide a back brief for my team. The outcome of this goal is to provide valuable intuition for team members that have no experience in game development or the use of Unreal Engine 4 and to better facilitate our learning curve.

Unreal Engine is a game engine developed by Epic Games and has been adopted in a variety of 3D games. It’s written in C++ and it requires a certain amount of knowledge in programming to be able to fully understand how to use the game engine.

I started off learning by watching Youtube tutorials, following the video closely step by step, first getting used to the interface and the main functions. It is very overwhelming, I watched over 12 hours of videos then another 10 hours of using the engine and it feels like I have barely scratched the surface.

During this week, I have created a sample level on what one of our levels may look like. I started off building the landscape then adjusting different shapes and objects to build decorations and platforms. Lastly, I gave the user character the ability to shoot fireballs, although I couldn’t get the animations to function properly, I believe more learning is required.
